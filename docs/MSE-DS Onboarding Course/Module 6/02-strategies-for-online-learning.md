# Strategies for Online Learning

**Speaker**: Ryan Miller, Director of the Office of Learning Resources at the Weingarten Center

## Transcript

As you begin coursework in an online environment, you may be anticipating some challenges ahead. Perhaps you're still sorting out how you'll complete readings and assignments, engage in research and demonstrate what you've learned, all while taking care of your many responsibilities at home, at work, and in the world. I'm Ryan Miller and I'm the Director of the Office of Learning Resources at the Weingarten Center. We are a Department within Penn's Office of the Vice Provost of University Life and we are a resource that is available to you throughout your studies at the University of Pennsylvania. Although you may never have taken a course online, this isn't necessarily the time to remake yourself as a learner. Instead, this is the time to be self-reflective, to set goals, and to figure out how your strengths apply in this setting.

We think you'll be most successful if you identify strategies that have worked in the past, either at work or in academic settings, and then, consider how these strategies might be adapted or further developed in specific ways for each course in your program. For instance, how do you usually tackle a big project with many steps? How might you go about reading a complicated text about an unfamiliar topic? What do you find are the best ways to communicate your ideas to others, particularly, when you are trying to persuade them to see your side of an argument? Such strategies will have application in your online coursework, but you'll also want to remain open to new methods for learning and showing what you know. For example, think about how you can engage with the readings, videos, and other materials in your online courses. Research on online learning has described how many students experience challenges when taking notes using digital technologies, with many citing the distractions of other online activities like email and social media notifications, making it difficult to concentrate on their course materials. Other research has highlighted how many students use digital technologies to transcribe every detail, instead of focusing on what's most important.

Now instead, think about taking minutes in a meeting, writing notes using a pen and paper, or making flashcards to study important concepts. These types of activities focus our attention on key details, on summarizing and synthesizing concepts, and on making connections with other ideas and experiences. At the Weingarten Center, we encourage you to take a similar approach when engaging with the materials in your online courses. Rather than transcribing what's said in a video, in which the full text is usually provided by closed captioning in our transcript, write a summary of what you've heard and questions you still have. When reading, use annotation software or print out the material to write questions or your own thoughts in response to the text. Then, take a moment to summarize each section after you've read it. After engaging with all of the materials for any given week, spend a little time to consider how what you've learned can connect with the other materials from the week, interact with other ideas from the course, and support your long-term professional goals. Additionally, it is essential that we learn how to interact in an online academic community in a way that provides support, acknowledgment, validation, knowledge and resource sharing, and collaboration.

Given that not being physically present reduces some of the instant person-to-person feedback that is traditionally part of classroom interaction and communication, we want to avoid you feeling isolated on a virtual island. There are two main benefits to contributing to an online community. First, it enhances your sense of participation, engagement, and growth. Being intentional about bringing your own identity, story, strengths, and curiosities into the online classroom. We believe that making your interactions with an online community of relevance to you will only enhance the community and that's what everyone gains from the course. Connecting your personal and professional experiences to the online curriculum is as important as any text that you may read. Second, widen your professional network.

Make a concerted effort to connect with your peers and professors, be intentional and thoughtful about identifying commonalities, differences, and opportunities for collective growth as you learn more about your peers and professors experiences, passions, and the questions that drive their inquiries. Such connections will only serve as a support infrastructure for your ongoing personal, academic, and professional development. We hope that these suggestions will be helpful as you become an active contributor in your online learning community.
